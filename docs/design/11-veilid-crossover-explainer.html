aiko chat · the other sea

A five-scene explainer

The other sea, and the chart we kept

Somewhere over the horizon was a whole sovereign network built for exactly our kind of chat. We sailed out to look it dead in the eye — and came home wiser, carrying a borrowed chart.

Scene 01

A rumour of a better sea

The idea

Word reaches the harbour of another sea entirely — not a single island but a whole living network of them, strangers’ boats and beacons all lit and talking, with no landlord anywhere in sight. And it was built for our exact trade: private conversation that belongs to the people having it. When a sea like that appears on the map, you don’t ignore it. You raise the spyglass and go take a proper look.

What we scouted: Veilid — a public peer-to-peer network whose flagship use was sovereign, no-server chat. The honest question it forced: do we cross over onto someone else’s sea, or keep sailing our own? Answering it well meant sailing out, not guessing from shore.
Scene 02

What was genuinely good out there

The idea

Up close, plenty to admire. Every boat carried its own flag with no harbour master issuing names — sovereignty, done and dusted. And it had a trick we hadn’t even drawn on our own charts: hidden sea-lanes, where a note could reach you without anyone watching being able to tell who was talking to whom. That last one stopped us cold. It was better than what we had. This was no toy; it was a serious sea.

The real draw: identity as a keypair, not an account — the same sovereignty aiko chat wants — plus private routing that hides who-talks-to-whom, a metadata-privacy capability we admired enough to want for ourselves. Its crypto choices were sound, too. Credit where due, before any verdict.
Scene 03

The buoy that couldn’t hold the crate

The idea

Then we tried to load our actual cargo. On that sea, a message waits for you in a little floating mailbox-buoy — elegant for a folded letter. But aiko chat carries crates: photos, video, the heavy things people really send. Hoist a crate toward that buoy and it simply won’t fit; you’d have to saw it into slivers and hope every sliver arrives. The mailbox was never built for freight, and our trade is freight.

The first honest limit: the network’s DHT mailbox holds only tiny, kilobyte-sized payloads — fine for text, wrong for rich media. Real photos and video need a proper warehouse: a blob store with signed fetch. And a warehouse on the water is exactly what an island is. Freight needs a dock, not a buoy.
Scene 04

The strongbox we’d still have to forge

The idea

There was a second catch. Crossing over doesn’t hand you a finished lock for your cargo — the seal that keeps a message readable only by its recipient is a strongbox you still have to forge yourself, hammer and anvil, on choppy young waters. That’s deep, exacting work whichever sea you’re on. So the network spared us none of the hardest labour while asking us to give up the harbour we already know how to keep lit.

The second honest limit: real end-to-end encryption — sealed so only the recipient can read — is costly to build no matter the substrate, and crossing over didn’t do that work for us. Adopting meant new dependence on a young sea while still forging the strongbox by hand. The trade stopped adding up.
Scene 05

Home harbour, borrowed chart

The idea

So we made the call, clear-eyed. We keep our own harbour — an island with a proper warehouse dock for the crates — because that’s what our trade actually needs. But we didn’t come home empty-handed. Rolled under the arm is a chart or two from that other sea: a couple of its cleverest ways of drawing keys and seals, kept as ideas we can cite and learn from. You look hard at another sea, and you take what teaches.

The verdict, earned: aiko chat builds its own island with a blob store for rich media, rather than cross over. What we keep from the voyage: the network’s sound crypto ideas, folded in as design citations — charts, not a dependency. Judgement, not a rejection.

The whole voyage, in one breath

We sailed to another sea, and chose our own harbour — wiser for the trip.

The network out there is real and good, and we admired it honestly: sovereign flags, hidden lanes, sound seals. But our trade carries crates, its little mailbox holds only letters, and the hardest lock was ours to forge either way. So we keep the harbour we know how to light — and we keep a borrowed chart or two.
