aiko chat · the harbourmaster's chart

A six-scene explainer

The chart of all the islands

You’re moored at one island. Out past its harbour lights are hundreds more — each with its own rooms and its own crew. This is the chart that shows you where they are, and how to sail in.

Scroll to explore
Scene 01

An island, and the rooms within it

The idea

Start with the shape of a place. An aiko community isn’t a single lonely room floating on the water — it’s a whole island, and the rooms are ashore inside it: a mess hall, a chart room, a quiet cove for two. You don’t join a room adrift; you join the island, and its rooms come with you. One landing, and you’re part of everything on it.

The two-level shape: a community is the island you join; its channels are the rooms it owns. Membership is two-level — join the community and you’re subscribed to its rooms, each carrying a community_id back to the island that holds it. A room is never an orphan on the open sea.
Scene 02

The harbourmaster keeps a chart

The idea

So how do you find islands you’re not on yet? You don’t sail blind and hope. There is a chart — a directory the harbourmaster keeps, a page of every island open to visitors: its name, what it’s about, and the bearing to reach it. Open the chart, read down the archipelago, and pick where to sail next. Discovery is a chart of islands, not a heap of loose rooms.

The discovery directory: the app asks the gateway for GET /v1/communities/discover and gets back a page of listed islands — name, description, and the coordinates the picker needs to reach each one via its gateway, the island’s protocol edge. Browsing happens at the community grain, exactly where a newcomer chooses a place.
Scene 03

Sorted by kind, ranked by life

The idea

A good chart isn’t a random pile. The harbourmaster groups the islands by what they’re for — makers here, music there, gardens down the coast — so you can head straight for your kind. And within a group the liveliest float to the top: the islands where the lanterns were lit most recently and the harbour holds the most boats. Honest signals, cheaply read.

Two simple sorts: islands are grouped by CommunityCategory — a closed set of kinds — and ranked by last_activity_at recency plus member_count. No opaque engagement model at the start, just “recently alive” and “well-crewed” — proxies a newcomer can trust and the chart can honestly show.
Scene 04

Only the open harbours are drawn

The idea

Not every island wants strangers sailing in, and the chart respects that. Only the islands that fly an open-harbour flag get drawn for all to see. A quiet island is reachable if a friend hands you its bearing, but it’s left off the public page. And the chart is drawn for you: a place you’ve turned away from, or one the harbourmaster has struck, simply isn’t inked on your copy.

Three postures, one gate: only public communities enter the directory; unlisted ones are joinable by direct link but never listed; private ones are invite-only. Every listing is filtered through the same per-viewer visibility predicate the moderation work already enforces — blocked or taken-down islands never appear on your chart.
Scene 05

The chart is last night’s; the mooring is now

The idea

Here’s the honest catch about any chart: it’s a snapshot from when it was drawn. Between the ink drying and your arrival, an island can pull up its welcome, close its harbour, or slip off the map entirely. So the chart never decides who gets in — the arrival does. You sail up, you hail, and the island itself answers. If it’s gone quiet since the chart was drawn, you’re turned away gently, never pushed through a door that already closed.

Join is the authority, not the listing: the directory page is a snapshot, so POST /v1/communities/{id}/join fails closed — if the island went private, was taken down, or vanished since it was listed, the gateway rejects and the app says “no longer available” rather than joining a place that just shut its harbour. Browse from the chart; be admitted only at the shore.
Scene 06

Ranked ashore, read aboard

The idea

One last division of labour, and it’s the reason the chart keeps getting better. The ordering — which islands rise to the top, and why — is decided ashore, by the harbourmaster, over the whole archipelago. Your boat never re-scores the sea; it just reads the ordered page and sorts it into the groups you asked for. So the harbourmaster can learn a smarter ordering — add “friends are here,” add “rising fast” — and every captain benefits without anyone reprinting their almanac.

Engine ashore, surface aboard: the relevance engine lives server-side behind the sort= projection; the app only groups and filters a server-ranked list — it never re-ranks. That seam is what lets ranking evolve as a gateway change alone, and it’s why a private signal like “friends here” can shape the order without the graph ever crossing to your boat.

The whole thing, in one breath

Islands own their rooms. A shared chart lists the open ones. You’re admitted at the shore, not by the ink.

Join an island and its rooms come with you. Find new ones on a chart the harbourmaster keeps — grouped by kind, the liveliest on top, only the open harbours drawn. Then let the arrival, not the listing, decide who sails in.

The chart never becomes a landlord: an island stays sovereign over who it welcomes, and the ordering is a role played ashore, free to grow smarter without ever reprinting a captain’s map. The same archipelago these islands belong to has a home of its own.
