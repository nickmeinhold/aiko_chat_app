A six-scene explainer

The flag, not the harbour

“Make phones the servers so we need no server” is the right instinct aimed at the wrong node. Your phone can’t be the harbour — but it can be the one thing a harbour never is: you.

Scroll to explore
Scene 01

The right instinct, the wrong node

The idea

You don’t want a landlord over your name. So the dream is “no server at all — let every phone be its own.” Hold onto the instinct and move it one boat over. Your phone can’t be the harbour that stays lit all night. But it can carry the one thing no harbour owns: your own flag — your name, your self, sailing with you wherever you land.

The reframe: the achievable, better version of “no server” is no server that knows who you are, and none you depend on for durability. Identity moves onto the phone; staying-awake-and-remembering stays on a node that is good at it. The phone holds the self; the node holds the waiting.
Scene 02

What a harbour is really for

The idea

Picture a harbour’s one defining job: to stay awake all night and answer a stranger who sails in while you sleep. That’s the whole trick of a server — be reachable, un-asked, at 3am. Now hold your daily phone up against that job. It wants to sleep the moment you pocket it, it has no address a stranger can dial, and a phone that’s off can hold no one’s mail. Three honest failures, and none of them are bugs.

The iOS wall, plainly: CGNAT hides millions of phones behind one address with no inbound port to dial; iOS suspends a backgrounded socket within seconds and offers no “keep listening” API; and store-and-forward for an offline friend simply is always-on durability. The daily phone fails a server’s defining job three ways at once.
Scene 03

Three jobs, split across two nodes

The idea

So how does any pocket app feel peer-to-peer if the phone can’t be a harbour? By noticing a server was never one job — it was three, bundled. Knowing who you are. Staying reachable and remembering. And keeping the one true order of what was said. Untie the bundle and hand each job to whoever’s built for it. Your flag — who you are — sails on the phone. The waiting and the remembering stay ashore.

The split: the phone carries identity + live presence; an always-on node carries durability + reachability. Because the phone never has to host the channel, it never takes on the multi-master state-merge that “every phone is a server” would drag back in — the mess Briar, Tox and SimpleX all wrestle. Two nodes, cleanly divided.
Scene 04

One logbook, and no arguments

The idea

Of the three jobs, one is sacred: keeping the true order of what was said. A channel needs exactly one harbourmaster whose logbook is the record — first this, then that, then this. Make every phone a server and you get a room full of logbooks that disagree, then a lifetime of arguing whose is right. aiko keeps a single logbook per channel on purpose, so there is simply nothing to reconcile.

The load-bearing rule: exactly one ordering authority per channel — a single total order the reconcile engine keeps. That one choice is how aiko sidesteps the multi-master state-resolution that peer-servers force, and it survives untouched here: the phone gains identity without ever becoming a second master. One logbook, no merge.
Scene 05

How a note reaches a sleeping boat

The idea

Follow one message out to a friend whose phone is asleep. First it tries the direct route — boat to boat, if both are on the water. If it can’t, it hands the sealed note to a relay who ferries it without ever reading it. And if the friend is truly ashore and dark, the note waits in a mailbox at the harbour, and a bell rings the moment their boat sails back in.

The delivery ladder, in order: direct connection first; then a blind relay that forwards without reading; then a store-and-forward mailbox on the always-on node. The bell is the push relay — the doorbell that wakes a suspended phone so it can come collect. Reachability without the phone ever staying awake.
Scene 06

You can be your own harbour

The idea

Here’s the escape hatch, for those who want it. Nothing says the always-on node must belong to someone else. A phone can be a harbour — just not the daily one in your pocket. A cheap little box on a shelf at home, plugged in and left awake, is a harbour of your own: it holds your mail and stays lit, while your daily phone stays free to sleep and simply carry the flag.

Sovereignty stays on the table: the durable node is a role, not a landlord — an old phone, a Raspberry Pi, a self-hosted island can each play it. The split doesn’t force you onto anyone’s server; it only says the durable role and the daily driver shouldn’t be the same device. Your harbour can be yours too.

The whole thing, in one breath

The self travels. The harbour waits. Neither has to be the other.

Stop asking the phone to be a server and it’s free to be something a server can’t: you. Identity sails in your pocket; durability stays lit ashore; one logbook keeps the truth. No landlord over your name, and no all-nighter asked of your battery.

The instinct behind “make every phone a server” — own yourself, owe no one — survives intact; it just moves onto the flag instead of the phone’s hardware. Who that flag is, and how an island decides to trust it, is the other half of the story: “Who Goes There” — identity & trust on the island.
