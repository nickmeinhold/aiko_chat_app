A six-scene explainer

One flag, every shore

Your name is a flag you carry, not a brand a harbour paints on you. And because you carry it, a whole federation of islands gets simpler, not more tangled.

Scroll to explore
Scene 01

A flag you carry, not a wall you’re painted on

The idea

Imagine your name sewn into a flag that only you can raise — you sail into any harbour and run it up the mast, and everyone knows you at a glance. That flag is yours. It doesn’t belong to the harbour, and no harbourmaster can take it down. Elsewhere it works the other way: your name is painted on one harbour’s wall, and the day that harbour crumbles or turns you away, your name goes with it.

The move — better by subtraction: your identity is a sovereign keypair you hold, owned by no island. Islands verify its signatures rather than issuing your name, so identity is island-independent from the start. Put the one piece of cleverness in the flag, and everything underneath gets to stay simple.
Scene 02

The lock at the dock guards the flag — it isn’t the flag

The idea

So how do you keep a flag that only you can raise? With the face-scan lock at your own dock — the one that opens only for you, and only here. It’s a brilliant guard precisely because it refuses to work at any other harbour, which is exactly why it can’t be the flag itself: a flag has to fly everywhere. So the lock does the honest thing — it opens the chest and lets your true flag out to sail.

The clean split: a passkey is deliberately domain-scoped — it only works at the one island it was made for, which is what makes it un-phishable and unfit to be a travelling identity. So the passkey is the guardian: it unlocks and authorizes your sovereign identity key (a device-held did:key), and that key is the passport that federates.
Scene 03

Every channel keeps one logbook

The idea

Now the flags need somewhere to talk. Each channel gets exactly one harbourmaster, and their logbook is the record: first this was said, then that, then this — one line after another, stamped in order. Everyone reads from that single book, so there is never a second version to argue over. The truth of what was said has one keeper, and only one.

The load-bearing rule: each channel is single-homed — one home island sequences its messages into a total order with ULID stamps. That’s what lets the whole design skip state resolution: with one sequencer per channel, there is no tangle of disagreeing copies to merge. The hard 80% of federating simply never has to exist.
Scene 04

Relay and verify, don’t replicate and resolve

The idea

You’re on your home island, but the conversation lives on a channel across the water. So your message sails over as a sealed note, still flying your flag. The far harbourmaster does two quick things: checks that the flag’s signature is really yours — asking your home island to vouch if you’re a stranger — and then stamps it into their one logbook. Your words arrive in order, and no two harbours ever fight over the record.

Federation is two small primitives: vouch (one island asks another “is this key really the holder you know?” and gets a signed yes) and relay (a note is forwarded to the channel’s home island, which checks the identity-key signature, assigns the ULID, and fans out). It’s relay-and-verify, never replicate-and-resolve — because no two islands are ever co-authoritative over one channel, there is no shared state to fight over.
Scene 05

The same flag on every shore

The idea

Here is the reward for carrying your own flag: you sail to a brand-new island and you’re still you — same flag, same history of who’s vouched for you, nothing to re-register. The name that took a decade for others to make portable is portable here by birth. And an honest word: the very freedom that lets your flag follow you also lets a flag that one harbour has barred simply raise itself at the next one — keeping troublemakers out across islands that don’t trust each other is the genuinely hard, still-open part.

What you gain, and the open edge: because the key is the identity, it is portable across islands natively — no migration, no re-issued name. The unsolved crack is cross-island moderation: a barred flag is free to reappear elsewhere, so a barring at one island doesn’t automatically travel. That’s the honest frontier this model has to earn — and it’s where reputation, not a headcount of humans, has to do the work.
Scene 06

One cheap habit keeps the whole map open

The idea

None of this has to be built today. There is exactly one small habit to keep from the very first day, while there is still only one island in the world: write every name with its home port attached. Not just “ava” but “ava, of the first harbour.” It costs nothing now, and it’s the one thing that’s a nightmare to add later — renumbering every soul under a live crowd. Keep that habit and every shore in this story stays reachable.

The one discipline paid up front: give users, channels, and communities globally namespaced IDs that carry their origin (id@home-island) even while there is a single island. Keep identity island-independent and never backslide into a flat, island-bound table. That near-zero-cost habit today is what keeps the entire federated future open — everything else stays deferred until it’s actually needed.

The whole thing, in one breath

Put the cleverness in the flag, and the sea underneath gets calm.

Identity is a key you hold, not a name a harbour lends you. Each channel keeps one logbook, so federating becomes relay-and-verify instead of a mesh of copies to reconcile. You carry one flag to every shore — and the one habit that keeps it all possible costs nothing but a home port written beside every name.

The flag proves you can raise it — but who that flag is, and how an island decides to trust it, is the other half of the story, told from the harbour’s side: “Who Goes There” — identity & trust on the island.
