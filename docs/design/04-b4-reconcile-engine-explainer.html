aiko chat · the reconcile engine

A five-scene explainer

Nothing to reconcile

When your boat sails back in, there is one master log to catch up against — so coming home is a quick reconcile, never an argument about whose story is true.

Scene 01

Coming home after a spell out of contact

The idea

A boat drops off the water mid-conversation — a robot on library wifi, a phone through a dead spot — then sails back in a minute later. That gap is not a disaster to this app; it is the normal passage. The moment the boat is back in the harbour, one small ritual runs: lay your own logbook beside the harbour’s and bring the two into step. A homecoming, not a crisis.

The whole job: the B4 reconcile pass is the ritual that runs on every return, walking a message through send → disconnect → reconnect → catch-up with nothing lost and nothing doubled. Dropping and rejoining is the main case the app is built around, not an edge it merely survives.
Scene 02

One master log, so there is nothing to argue

The idea

Why is the catch-up quick instead of a quarrel? Because the harbour keeps exactly one master log for the channel — first this was said, then that, then this — and everyone agrees it is the record. Reconciling against a single ordered ledger is just reading down it until you reach today. There are no two versions to weigh, no whose-story-wins, nothing to stitch together.

The load-bearing rule: one ordering authority per channel keeps a single total order. Because the order is decided in one place, catching up has nothing to merge — the reconcile engine reads the sequence, it never negotiates it.
Scene 03

A ribbon marking exactly where you left off

The idea

You don’t re-read the whole log from page one every homecoming — that would be slow and pointless. The harbourmaster keeps a ribbon tucked at the last line you and the log had already agreed on. Catch-up starts right at the ribbon and runs forward: read the new lines below it, then move the ribbon down to today. Everything above the ribbon you already hold; only what is below needs fetching.

The bookmark: a per-channel cursor — the watermark the reconcile pass advances — marks the last entry known to be contiguous. Catch-up fetches forward from the cursor, then moves it up. One writer owns that ribbon, so it can never point at a line the boat hasn’t truly filled in yet.
Scene 04

A clean line between the written and the live

The idea

There is one seam that has to be exact, or a message could slip through it. When the boat re-hails the harbour, the harbourmaster names a single line in the log: everything up to here is already written down — go read it from the ledger. Everything after here will come to you live, called out across the water as it happens. A buoy marks the line, and nothing falls in the gap because there is no gap.

The handoff fence: on re-subscribe the island answers with a fence — the newest entry at that instant. Everything ≤ fence is caught up from written history; everything > fence arrives on the live stream. The two partitions meet exactly at the buoy, so there is no gap and no overlap.
Scene 05

Say it twice, it’s logged once

The idea

One more worry to put to rest. A boat that lost contact mid-send can’t be sure its last line ever reached the log, so on return it simply says it again. The harbourmaster doesn’t panic or write a second entry — he sees it already sits in the log under the same mark, ticks it off, and moves on. Repeating yourself is safe by design: the same line lands in the same place, every time.

Idempotent catch-up: a re-sent message carries its original mark, so the log recognises it and keeps the same entry rather than minting a new one. The reconcile pass can re-send freely and read forward freely and still land on exactly one row — no duplicate, no loss, whatever order things arrive.

The whole thing, in one breath

One true order to come home to, so the catch-up is quick and the truth is never in doubt.

Because a channel keeps a single ordered log, a boat that drops and rejoins never has to argue its way back in. It reads forward from a ribbon, meets the live stream at a clean fence, and re-says anything it isn’t sure of — and lands on exactly one true record. Reconciling turns out to be the easy part, precisely because there is only ever one order to reconcile against.

The magic isn’t a clever merge algorithm — it’s the absence of one. Give every channel a single harbourmaster whose log is the order, and the hardest problem in distributed chat — whose version of events is real — simply never gets asked. The reconcile engine spends its cleverness on the return journey, not on refereeing the truth.
