Design 11 — Veilid: the cross-over evaluation

DECISION 2026-07-14 (Nick): Veilid is not the substrate. Rich media — images and video — is a first-class product requirement, and a serverless beta DHT-mailbox overlay is the wrong delivery path for large binary blobs: DHT records are kilobyte-scale, so media forces lossy out-of-band chunked transfer over exactly the app_call layer flagged beta (message loss, no notifications, worse on cellular). Rich media needs a real content store (object storage + signed fetch), which is island territory. Path: build our own island with a blob store. No adopt, no cross-over-the-network. What survives this decision: the VLD0 crypto precedent already folded into the identity crucible (a design citation, never a network dependency). What it costs: metadata-privacy via private-routing (§5 "expand", task #28) loses its only cheap path and now needs its own answer. What stays live regardless: background delivery / push-wake (#29) — media makes notifications matter more. The evaluation below is retained as the reasoning of record.

Veilid (Cult of the Dead Cow, DEF CON 31, 2023) is a Rust P2P framework — "Tor + IPFS" — with official Flutter bindings, and its flagship example app VeilidChat is a Flutter, end-to-end-encrypted, serverless chat. That is almost exactly aiko's use case, built intentionally: chat was Veilid's primary design target. This doc asks the uncomfortable, exciting question — should aiko build on Veilid, steal from it, or ignore it? — grounds the answer in Veilid's own docs, and lands a recommendation the tasks below can execute.

1 · The thesis — our endgame, already built

Veilid is the productized, battle-tested version of aiko's sovereign-federation endgame. Node identity is a keypair (our Design 06 / task #21). Messages are signed by the sender and encrypted per-recipient, stored in a multi-writer DHT as a mailbox read on reconnect (our #24 untrusted-integrity + #25 untrusted-confidentiality, combined). Its "relay/capable node" is our island. And it ships the one capability we don't even have on the roadmap: onion-style private routing — metadata privacy. It is not inspiration; it is a reference implementation of most of what we intend to build.

The alignment is not coincidence. Veilid was designed with chat as the primary use case, so the fit with aiko is intentional, not lucky. That cuts two ways — it validates our architecture and forces an honest question about differentiation (§6).

Why the two-key split we already have is not an accident

A recurring temptation is "why not use the passkey for everything?" Veilid's design answers it from first principles, the same way our codebase does: WebAuthn passkeys cannot sign arbitrary bytes — the private key is non-extractable and locked inside the ceremony/origin. So you cannot use a passkey to sign messages or DID assertions. That is exactly why aiko carries a separate native Ed25519 sovereign key (lib/services/sovereign_key_store.dart). Veilid's VLD0 crypto suite makes the same call: separate Ed25519 (identity/signing) and X25519 (key agreement), plus BLAKE3, XChaCha20-Poly1305, Argon2id — a domain-matched precedent for the identity crucible's key-material decision (a separate X25519 key, not a birational conversion).

2 · The physics it does not repeal — the availability anchor

My initial claim in the session was "phones are the worst possible DHT nodes." True for vanilla Kademlia (BitTorrent/IPFS): phones can't accept inbound connections, churn IPs, and get their sockets killed by the OS. Veilid is the counterexample — but note precisely how it wins, because it is the punchline of our whole topology:

Veilid does not make the phone a good DHT node. It splits the role: phones are lightweight participants reachable via relays and private routes (the receiver publishes a route, not an IP; NAT is traversed by the overlay), while higher-capability nodes carry routing and storage. The availability anchor — the thing that must be awake when your phone is asleep — is not eliminated. It is turned into a permissionless, swappable relay role that anyone can run. That is exactly our "demote the gateway to a replaceable untrusted relay" conclusion, shipped since 2023. Our island is Veilid's relay/capable node.

No cryptography repeals "someone must be awake." Veilid just made the awake-thing anonymous, permissionless, and — because messages are signed and encrypted — trustless. Persistence collapses from a trust problem into a replication problem. That is the whole prize the sovereign-signing work already bought us.

3 · The make-or-break — no background delivery

The single question that decides adopt-vs-cross-over is: how does a serverless chat receive a message on iOS when backgrounded? iOS kills background sockets, so there is no always-on connection to receive a DHT-mailbox write in real time. Veilid's own Known Issues page (verified 2026-07-13) answers it plainly:

"Notifications are not enabled yet. You have to open the app and check for new messages." — plus documented "lag, invitations that don't complete, slow message sending, message loss, repeated messages," and "more problematic on cellular than wifi." VeilidChat is beta.

This is the availability-anchor physics wearing an iOS hat: the phone must not only have a relay holding its message, it must be woken to receive it. Even the best-engineered serverless chat has not beaten this — it defers delivery to app-open, putting the pain on the user rather than infrastructure. The honest consequence: our own islands hit the same wall (task #29). "Build our own" does not escape it. The realistic escape is a minimal push anchor (APNs/FCM) that carries only a wake signal, not content — E2EE keeps that relay blind to bodies.

Discipline: predict from the instrument, not the reputation. The adopt bet earns a measured spike (network reliability, Flutter-binding maturity, does-it-fit-robots), not enthusiasm.

Aside — why DHT-for-storage is still wrong for us, and where DHT is right

A vanilla DHT is a distributed index, not distributed storage or an always-on host: it tells you which node holds content X; a node still has to be storing it and awake. It is worst exactly where chat lives — private, low-fanout content (your 3am DM has one pinner) evaporates without an anchor; lookups take seconds; participation leaks metadata. Both Nostr and SSB — the two systems closest to our design — evaluated DHT-for-messages and chose relays instead. The DHT's right layer is relay discovery (a find-the-node problem), not message storage. Veilid threads this needle with a signed, multi-writer, relay-backed DHT — closer to "replicated mailbox on capable nodes" than to raw Kademlia.

6 · The differentiation — what aiko is that VeilidChat isn't

If VeilidChat already exists as the reference sovereign E2EE chat, the honest question is: what is aiko? The answer is in the memory graph already, and adopting Veilid makes it cleaner, not moot:

Robots are first-class citizens. VeilidChat is human-to-human. aiko's differentiator is AI participants as group members holding keys — and E2EE (#25) makes that literal: the bot is a member with a keypair, not a server reading plaintext (Design 09: reputation-not-personhood; robots are participants aiko exists to include). So the coherent story is not "build a VeilidChat competitor" — it's adopt the substrate, differentiate at the social/AI layer: Veilid gives sovereign transport + E2EE + metadata privacy; aiko adds robot-citizens + reputation + product. That division is cleaner than building the substrate ourselves.

7 · Recommendation + open questions

Cross-over now; spike adopt as a measured evaluation; reject nothing. Steal VLD0 crypto (done, in the identity crucible), the DHT-mailbox pattern (#12), and private-route-lite (#28) today. Do not bet production aiko on the live Veilid network while background delivery is unsolved and the network is young (#26). Re-open the adopt arm when Veilid ships notifications and the overlay matures — at which point it is the single biggest roadmap collapse available and delivers the "better than Matrix" claim literally.

Open questions to deepen the evaluation (task #26):

  • Mobile background delivery — the decider. Read the Veilid roadmap (Discord/GitLab) for whether/when notifications land. Our own islands need the same answer (#29).
  • Group E2EE maturity — VeilidChat's multi-writer group model vs MLS. Channel chat is group chat; this is the hard part of #25.
  • Network reliability at our scale — measured, not assumed. The "beta / message loss / cellular" caveats are the instrument reading to trust.
  • Does the DHT-mailbox admit a robot member cleanly? — the differentiation (§6) rests on it.
