Design 09 — Sybil resistance: reputation, not personhood

A self-minted keypair is free, so "everyone is their own root of identity" is also "anyone can be ten thousand roots." That is the one place sovereign identity creates a problem instead of solving one — and it is where a "better than Matrix" story is earned or lost. This design argues that aiko should not try to solve global proof-of-personhood at all. It sacrifices the global-uniqueness corner of a well-attested trilemma — the one corner a substrate-agnostic chat app never needed — and defends the real threat (per-island spam, abuse, impersonation) with per-island economic cost gradients denominated in the one scarce resource neither a human nor an AI can multiply: reputation someone will lend you and can lose.

1 · The impossibility frontier solid

Sybil resistance is governed by the Decentralized Identity Trilemma: you cannot simultaneously maximise sybil-resistance, self-sovereignty (no trusted third party), and privacy. Two peer-reviewed primary sources state it verbatim (Frontiers in Blockchain 2020 / arXiv 2008.05300), and independent reviews go further — "there is not any known design that fulfils all three criteria."

It is a cost frontier, not a proven theorem. The honest word is unsolved, not impossible: nobody has built all three, but no one has proven you can't. That distinction is the difference between "give up" and "choose which corner to underfund."

The deepest reason is a regress — "who verifies the verifier?" Any objective cryptographic personhood check bottoms out in a trusted attestor, and verifying them needs another, forever. Every escape hatch pays one corner: a trusted issuer (CanDID deduplicates on SSN/Aadhaar inside MPC — recovers privacy, pays decentralization), a scarce resource / stake (PoW/PoS — concentrate into plutocracy), biometrics (Worldcoin — pays privacy + centralization), or a social graph (keeps decentralization, pays reliable sybil-resistance).

Why personhood is a category error for aiko

Proof-of-personhood's formal definition requires a substrate that is "easy for humans to produce, but difficult for Artificial Intelligence to replicate" (arXiv 2008.05300). AI-hardness is a defining requirement of personhood. Point any personhood mechanism at aiko's user base and it does not filter spam — it filters the very participants aiko exists to include. A robots-first-class app cannot adopt a bouncer whose entire job is to keep robots out. Rejecting personhood is therefore forced by the values, not a preference.

The three words that get conflated, and shouldn't. Personhood = a claim about a body in the world ("this key is a distinct human"); points outside the system; needs an oracle. Uniqueness = the one-per-person property personhood exists to deliver; only strictly needed for one-person-one-vote (governance, UBI). Reputation = a claim about the key's past behaviour inside the system; earned, not verified; and the only one of the three that never asks what you are. A spam-bot and a spam-human are both just "untrusted young keys behaving badly" — and when robots are citizens, not having to tell them apart is exactly the property you want.

3 · The aiko design — the stake is a bet you place plausible

Every possessable denomination — money, a device attestation, account-age — fails the robots economics test the same way: a tireless AI agent can possess it at scale as cheaply as a human. The only irreducibly scarce, substrate-agnostic resource is reputation someone will lend you and can lose.

The stake is not a thing you have — it's a bet you place. An existing member co-signs a newcomer and stakes their own standing on that newcomer's behaviour. Vouchee abuses ⇒ voucher is slashed too. This inverts the web-of-trust refutation: that critique assumed vouching is free (endorse, risk nothing) — making fake friends is cheap. Bond it and the attack becomes "recruit real stakers willing to bet their standing on you," which is exactly as scarce for an AI farm as for a human one.

Stake all the way up — this is what removes the token

The same denomination recurses through the federation: islands stake their federation standing (admit spammers ⇒ peers defederate — the Mastodon/Matrix model); members stake island standing to vouch; the root is the island operator (the genesis voucher — which is also the cold-start seed, see §7). Reputation is non-fungible and can't be minted or bought, only earned — which is precisely what defeats the money-attack and the AI-farm-attack in one stroke. No native token is required.

The composition — layered onto RLN, buildable

The stake is not one thing; it's a blend, and each layer maps onto a distinct part of the threat. The design choice is deliberate: make the AI-cheap resource the weak layer and the substrate-agnostic one carry the sybil-count bound.

4 · Adversarial hardening plausible

The sock-puppet ring → root-anchoring + conserved vouching

An attacker mints keys that vouch for each other, aging together, manufacturing mutual standing with no external staker — the "non-intersecting groups" attack wearing a bonded hat. It is defeated only by two properties together:

  • Root-anchored trust (EigenTrust / PageRank teleporting to island operators): a closed ring with no inflow from real roots has ≈0 standing regardless of internal vouching.
  • Conserved vouching: a vouch transfers standing from a finite budget — it never mints it. This is necessary because trust propagates transitively (PageRank) while slashing is pairwise; without conservation, one real edge lets a puppet bootstrap the ring and absorb the slashing internally (attacker slashing attacker = no deterrent). With it, a ring can never hold more total standing than flowed in from real roots.
The punchline. A reputation system doesn't prevent sybils — it makes them pay the full price of good behaviour, at which point they are indistinguishable from, and as useful as, real members. To run 10,000 sybils you must have earned 10,000 sybils' worth of root-anchored standing, i.e. behaved well at 10,000-scale — operationally identical to being 10,000 good users. The system never has to tell a farm from a crowd: a farm that paid full price in good behaviour is a crowd. Same move as robots-first-class, one level up — don't care human/AI (personhood), don't care one/many (uniqueness), only whether you paid.

The grey market → self-defusing reputation

Everything valuable gets a grey market; aged accounts sell today, so "non-transferable" is too strong. The stronger, honest version: an aged Reddit account's value is intrinsic (the karma travels with the login); a bonded-vouch reputation's value is extrinsic — it lives in the vouchers' continued willingness to back you, and they're slashed if you misbehave. On change-of-hands + behaviour-shift, the bonds unwind. You buy a reputation that evaporates on first misuse — and misuse is the only reason to buy it. A bomb that defuses itself the instant you try to detonate it. The residual is low-and-slow abuse under the detection threshold — not a cannon, and exactly the point where abuse becomes uneconomical.

5 · Honest boundaries plausible

The partial reclaim on coordinated influence

Reputation-cost crushes spam (high-volume, detectable ⇒ bonds unwind). Its weak point is low-volume coordinated influence — an astroturf farm that behaves "well" by the rules but acts in concert never trips behavioural slashing. But behavioural detection is only one instrument on the graph. Structural detection is another: an organic member earns diverse root-inflow scattered across the graph; a coordinated farm shares a few entry edges and vouches mostly internally ⇒ dense-internal, thin-to-roots ⇒ a low-conductance cut visible even when every member behaves perfectly. To hide structurally, the farm must acquire genuine diverse root-inflow — pay the full price again. Two orthogonal instruments on one graph: behavioural (spam) + conductance (the coordinated farm).

Correction from the verified lit pass (2026-07-17, §6.1): conductance detection must be scoped as local whitelisting around the trust root, never a global classifier. The refutation is unanimous and specific: community structure defeats universal conductance-based detection — the cut between two tightly-knit honest communities can be as sparse as the honest–sybil cut, so a sybil ring is indistinguishable from a legitimate tight sub-community (Alvisi et al., IEEE S&P 2013: "these considerations argue against universal sybil defense"). Detection accuracy anticorrelates with modularity at −0.81 across eight real networks (Viswanath et al.), and the fast-mixing assumption fails worst on small acquaintance-based graphs — exactly an island's profile (mixing walks of 200–400 measured vs the 10–15 assumed; Mohaisen et al., IMC 2010). The guarantee degrades linearly with mixing time rather than cliff-failing (Yu 2011), and the root-anchored formulation this design already uses (§4) is the local-whitelisting form — so the instrument survives, but only in that form, and its per-island tuning is an open engineering question, not a solved one.
The ceiling is honest, not a regression. No sybil-resistance scheme fully solves coordinated influence — a room of verified-unique humans can still brigade. So reputation gives up nothing personhood would have bought here. Coordinated-influence detection is a separate instrument (behavioural correlation), orthogonal to the stake question.

The costs I'm not hiding

Root-anchoring is a mild per-island re-centralization (the operator is a trust root) and reintroduces cold-start gatekeeping (you need someone established to co-sign). Federation softens this — join a more-open island, earn standing, port it — but does not erase it. The framing that keeps it consistent with the whole thesis: each island is a trust root for its own members; there is no global root. Decentralization is preserved at the federation level, not the individual level — the same corner-choice as sacrificing global uniqueness.

7 · Open problems thin

  • Genesis / bootstrapping — who vouches the first island operator, and how does a brand-new island earn federation standing from zero? Loops back to Design 05 / the island directory: island discovery is the federation cold-start surface. (Next exploration.)
  • Whose reputation is it — this doc says "member" and "voucher" without pinning the identity model those words rest on. Resolved (as a candidate frame) in Design 12 — the identity graph: Participant / Principal / Self / Owner reconciliation, the bot-cardinality fork, and why every stake and slash here is an operation on the Principal graph only.
  • Prior-art pass — partially done 2026-07-17 (§6.1): staked-vouching (TrustDavis) and SybilGuard/SybilLimit small-graph behaviour settled 3-0. Still open: Advogato's real-world attack history, Friedman–Resnick's exact whitewashing result, EigenTrust teleport parameterization, and whether anyone has composed reputation-gated registration with RLN (Waku RLN-relay / Semaphore ecosystems).
  • Measure, don't assume, the island graph — new from the lit pass: the structural instrument's strength depends on the island's measured mixing time and modularity (linear degradation, no cliff). A small tool that computes these on a real island's vouch graph would turn §5's corrected caveat into an engineering dial.
  • Stake denomination detail — for a no-token, phone-first, gateway-relayed app, what does the "registration stake" concretely resolve to? Device-attestation vs time-bond vs proof-of-prior-good-behaviour vs vouch-stake, and the app↔gateway split for each. The app produces the credential; the gateway is where it's verified, staked, and slashed.
  • Conserved-trust budget mechanics — how vouch-capacity replenishes with earned standing; the exact EigenTrust teleport parameter; how slashing propagates back through a transitive trust path.
