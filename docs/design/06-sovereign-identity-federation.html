Design 06 — Sovereign-identity federation

A federation model that beats Matrix by subtraction: move the one piece of cleverness to identity (sovereign, key-rooted, owned by no gateway), and the rest of the system gets simpler than Matrix, not more complex — single-homed channels keep their total order, federation becomes relay-and-verify instead of replicate-and-resolve, and identity becomes portable, the thing Matrix has failed to solve for a decade.

Status: vision, v2 platform-tier — NOT a build spec and NOT on the launch path. Captured 2026-06-29 (task #29). Companion to Design 05, which is the near-term community model built federation-ready so this stays possible. See also Design 07 — notifications, the one place this model meets a platform wall (APNs/FCM) that subtraction can't remove — and Design 08, which puts this sovereign identity on the phone and lets an always-on island carry only durability (the answer to "can't we drop the server entirely?").
Confidence legend: solid grounded in what's built · plausible coherent but unvalidated · thin genuinely unsolved, where I'd bet but wouldn't trust me.

1. The thesis — better by subtraction

You don't beat Matrix by out-replicating it. You beat it by not replicating at all — putting the cleverness in sovereign identity, and letting everything underneath stay a single-master, totally-ordered, boringly simple system. Matrix is complex because it refused to make identity sovereign; fix that one thing and the complexity it was buying becomes unnecessary.

Two columns are worse for us (durability, moderation) and the rest are better. §7 is honest about the two worse ones.

2. What Matrix gets wrong: server-bound identity

solid In Matrix, @alice:example.org welds Alice's identity to a homeserver. If that server dies, vanishes, or de-platforms her, she loses her identity, her history, and her social graph. Account portability (moving @alice to another server with her rooms intact) has been an open proposal for years and remains unshipped. Every heavy piece of Matrix — the event-signing, the federation API, state resolution — exists to make a network of servers-as-trust-units agree. The server is the atom of trust, and that's the original sin.

3. The three layers

  SOVEREIGN IDENTITY   a keypair you hold. Owned by no gateway. Verifiable everywhere.
        │  (authenticates to / is vouched by)
        ▼
  GATEWAY DEPLOYMENT   a homeserver-equivalent. Hosts channels, relays federation.
        │  (homes)
        ▼
  CHANNEL (single-homed)   one authoritative gateway sequences it with ULIDs.
        ▲
        │  (logical membership, by global ID, across gateways)
  COMMUNITY   a federated overlay (Design 05's Community / a Matrix "Space"),
              referencing channels homed on possibly-different gateways.

The app, as in Design 05, only ever talks to its own home gateway over one WSS connection. Federation is strictly gateway↔gateway; the client never multi-connects. A channel homed elsewhere is reached through the home gateway's relay.

4. Identity — sovereign key, passkey-protected

The nuance that makes this honest, not hand-waving: a passkey is not the federated identity key, and can't be. Passkeys are deliberately domain-scoped (a credential for chat.imagineering.cc is, by design, unusable at any other origin — that's the phishing resistance). So a passkey can't directly be a cross-gateway identity. solid

plausible The clean separation:

  • Sovereign identity key — a device-held keypair (secure enclave / DID-style, e.g. did:key). This is the portable identity. Gateways verify its signatures. It is the thing that is "you," everywhere.
  • Passkey — the device-side root that protects and authorizes the identity key: it authenticates you to a gateway and signs a delegation binding "this session / this gateway may act for identity key K." Passkeys become the on-ramp and the guardian, not the passport itself.

This reframes the auth pivot (passkeys-primary, #21) as more than a login: it's the device-rooted base of a sovereign identity system. The passkey unlocks/authorizes the portable key; the portable key is what federates.

Signed-message shape (sketch) 
// A message is signed by the sovereign identity key, relayed by gateways unchanged.
{
  "channel":   "<global channel id>",     // namespaced: id@homeGateway
  "author":    "<identity public key / did>",
  "ulid":      "<assigned by the channel's HOME gateway>",  // single sequencer
  "body":      { ... },
  "sig":       "<identity-key signature over (channel, author, body, prev?)>"
}

The home gateway assigns the ULID (preserving total order) and verifies sig against the author's identity key — which it learned either because the author is local, or via a federation vouch from the author's home gateway. No state resolution: one gateway sequences, everyone else relays + verifies.

5. Why channels stay single-homed (B4 survives)

solid This is the deliberate refusal that buys all the simplicity. Each channel has exactly one home gateway that sequences its messages with ULIDs. That means:

  • The per-channel total order the entire B4 reconcile engine depends on — the reconnect fence (latest ULID), before/after cursor paging, the historyContiguousThrough watermark — is untouched. There is still one sequencer per channel; it just might not be your gateway.
  • No DAG, no prev_events merge, no state resolution. The hardest 80% of Matrix simply does not exist here.
  • The cost we accept (§7): a channel's history lives on its home gateway. If that gateway dies, the channel's history is at risk — recovered by ordinary backup of a single-master log, not by live multi-master replication.
The load-bearing trade, stated plainly: we give up replication-based durability to keep total order and delete state resolution. For a chat product pre-scale, a backed-up single-home log is a vastly better cost/complexity point than a live consensus mesh. If a specific channel ever needs Matrix-grade survival, replication can be added as a later opt-in layer on top — it is not a foundation we must pour now.

6. Federation = relay-and-verify, not replicate-and-resolve

plausible Server-to-server reduces to two primitives, both far lighter than Matrix's federation API:

  • Vouch: gateway B asks gateway A "is this identity key really the holder you know as X?" — A answers with a signed assertion. (Replaces Matrix's user-resides-on-server coupling with a portable-key attestation.)
  • Relay: a message authored by a remote user for a channel homed on gateway H is forwarded to H, which verifies the identity-key signature, assigns the ULID, and fans out. Remote readers receive it via their home gateway relaying from H.

There is no shared state to resolve because no two gateways are co-authoritative over the same channel. Conflict resolution — the thing that makes Matrix hard — is structurally absent.

7. The hard problems (honest map)

Three cracks. I'd rather name them than sell around them; two are where I'd genuinely not trust my own optimism.

thin Cross-federation moderation is the scary one. Block + takedown (#7) is single-gateway today and clean. Sovereign identity means a user blocked on gateway A can present the same key on gateway B and appear in a shared community. Reputation, block-propagation, and takedown across mutually-distrusting deployments is unsolved-grade — Matrix struggles badly here too. This is the problem most likely to gate whether the whole vision is viable, and it has no cheap answer. It must be designed before any real multi-gateway traffic, not after.

7.1 A candidate direction: accountability via staked vouching thin

The sybil/moderation gate has the same root in both 05 and here: a sovereign key is free to mint, so authenticity (this message came from holder-of-K) buys no accountability (K is a real, rate-limitable human). One direction worth researching turns the social-recovery graph (§9) into double duty: the guardians who hold your key shares also stake their standing on your conduct. Minting a key stays free; minting a key others will federate with costs your social graph's skin. Accountability then flows backward through the vouch edges — the accountable unit becomes "a key plus the reputation staked behind it," and that is the one cost a sybil farmer cannot fake at scale.

This is not new ground — the lineage is old and, in one case, Nobel-validated:

PrecedentMechanismLesson
Frankpledge (medieval England)A tithing of ~10 households was collectively liable for each member's conduct — produce the absconder or pay."Friends punished for your behaviour" ran English law for centuries; it localised an impossible monitoring problem. Also bred guilt-by-association abuse.
Suretyship / bailA third party forfeits a stake if you abscond.The individualised form of the same primitive; still in use.
Grameen joint-liability lending (Yunus, Nobel 2006)Group-liable microloans, no collateral — peer monitoring substitutes for a credit bureau.The empirical proof it can produce cooperation at scale rather than collapse. The reference with data.
Delegation-slashing (delegated PoS)Delegators backing a misbehaving validator lose their delegated stake.The cleanest production "backers punished for the principal" mechanism today.
DeSoc / Soulbound Tokens (Weyl, Ohlhaver, Buterin 2022)Non-transferable reputation as collateral, recovery via community.The most on-point modern paper; ties recovery guardians to a reputation graph. Pair with sybil-graph detection (SybilRank, EigenTrust) for the signal.
thin One design error from a social-credit system. Three failure modes must be designed against, not hoped away: (1) contagion griefing / guilt-by-association — a bad actor deliberately poisons their whole cluster (frankpledge's documented abuse); (2) chilling effect & exclusion of the vulnerable — if vouching is risky, people vouch only for the already-safe, freezing out newcomers and thin networks (the mechanism that secures the core ossifies the boundary); (3) coercion & collusion — a principal coerces vouchers, or a cluster colludes to defect together (Grameen fights this structurally: small groups + frequent repayment, not good intentions). The specific synthesis — recovery guardians also being slashable accountability sureties — appears genuinely under-deployed; treat it as a seam to invent in, with these three as the design's load-bearing constraints.
  • plausible Key loss = identity loss. Pure keypair identity is unforgiving. Passkey sync (iCloud Keychain / Google Password Manager) mitigates device loss, and the identity-key / passkey split (§4) allows a recovery/rotation ceremony (re-authorize a new identity key from a recovered passkey) — but that ceremony is unbuilt and must be deliberate. Matrix at least lets a homeserver assist recovery; sovereign identity shifts that burden to the protocol. This is the crack with the most existing research — see §9; threshold signatures arguably dissolve it rather than solve it.
  • plausible Durability. The single-home trade (§5) — solvable with backups, but it is a property surrendered, and a gateway operator's backup discipline becomes a user-facing reliability factor.

8. What this asks of the present — one cheap principle

Keep identity gateway-independent, and namespace IDs globally — from day one. Passkeys already make identity a key rather than a server-address, so this is mostly "don't backslide into a flat, gateway-bound user table." Give users, channels, and communities globally namespaced IDs (carrying origin gateway) even while there is exactly one gateway. That single discipline keeps this entire future open at near-zero present cost; a flat ID space retrofitted under a live user base is the migration from hell (it's the one thing Matrix unambiguously got right early).

Explicitly deferred (do not build pre-launch, do not let it touch the launch path): the vouch/relay protocol, the identity-key/passkey delegation ceremony, cross-gateway moderation, the recovery story, single-home backup tooling. Each is its own future design. Design 05 is the near-term build; it adopts only the cheap principle above and ships independently of everything in this document.

9. Prior art & research threads

The two unbuilt parts §7 worries about — recovery / key-loss and multi-device key management — are not greenfield. There is a deep, partly-shipping body of work, and pointing at it is more honest than the hand-wavy "recovery ceremony" §4 gestured at. The single reframe that matters: don't store one portable key and try to protect it — make the key never-whole and distributed.

9.1 Splitting a key across devices / people

ThreadWhat it isRelevance
Shamir's Secret Sharing (Shamir, 1979)k-of-n split of a secret; any k shares reconstruct it.The naive answer. Fatal flaw for identity: the key must be reassembled on one device to use — a momentary single point of compromise. Good for cold backup, wrong for a live signing key.
Threshold signatures (TSS) — FROST (Komlo & Goldberg, Schnorr), GG18/GG20 (Gennaro–Goldfeder, ECDSA)Each holder keeps a share; they jointly emit a signature via MPC. The key is never assembled — ever.The real answer. Nothing to steal at rest or in use; lose a device and the remaining k still sign, then re-share to a new one. Collapses recovery and multi-device into one mechanism.
Distributed Key Generation (DKG) — Pedersen; FROST-DKGThe key is born split; no whole key ever existed.Removes the "dealer" trust problem — no one (not even the user's first device) ever held the whole key.
Verifiable / Proactive secret sharing — Feldman, Pedersen VSS; Herzberg et al.Holders verify their share without trusting the dealer; shares are periodically refreshed.A device compromised slowly over months only ever yields stale fragments. Hardens the "friends' phones" model against patient attackers.
Social-recovery wallets — Argent; Buterin's "guardians" essay (2021); ERC-4337 account abstractionTrusted contacts ("guardians") authorize recovery of a lost key.The productized, user-legible framing of "store pieces on people you trust." Shipping in crypto wallets today.
MPC custody products — ZenGo, Fireblocks, Coinbase WaaS, Web3AuthThreshold signatures, productized.Evidence this is engineering, not theory — though most split user↔server, not user↔friends.

9.2 Sovereign identity protocols (the closest spiritual sibling to this design)

  • KERI (Key Event Receipt Infrastructure, Sam Smith) — the most relevant thread by far. Self-certifying, host-independent identifiers with built-in key rotation and witnesses — which are almost exactly this design's federation vouch primitive (§6). If 06 has a sibling already in the wild, it's KERI.
  • W3C DIDs (did:key, did:peer) — the standardized envelope for the sovereign identifier the model assumes in §4.
thin What none of this fixes: the sybil / moderation gate (§7). Threshold signatures and social recovery harden custody — loss, theft, portability, multi-device. They do nothing for accountability: a threshold key is still free to mint, so "split across friends' phones" makes your identity robust without making a spammer's identity scarce. That is a proof-of-personhood problem (Worldcoin, BrightID, Proof of Humanity — all contested), categorically separate from key storage, and it remains the viability gate.
