Scene 01

The logbook you keep aboard

The idea

Every boat worth its salt keeps its own logbook — not a borrowed copy of the harbour’s, but its own, written as things happen and readable by lamplight when you’re a week from any signal. aiko chat gives your phone the same thing: a small store on board that holds the whole conversation, so the app opens and reads true whether or not there’s a tower in sight.

The drift cache is that on-board log: an on-device SQLite store (the drift package) holding the one coherent local truth the UI renders. Screens read from reactive watchChannel streams, never straight off the wire — so the boat runs the same, signal or none.
Scene 02

One message, two names

The idea

The moment you send a line, you chalk it into the log under a quick working name — enough to find it again in a heartbeat. When it reaches the harbour and is entered in the official register, it earns a second, permanent name. Same line, two names across its life: the one you gave it in a hurry, and the one the world will know it by.

A message carries a client clientTempId (a uuid) at compose time and a server serverUlid once acknowledged. The one frame that carries BOTH — the ack — is the sole join between your working name and the harbour’s register. Everything reconcile does hangs off that single slip.
Scene 03

When your own words sail back

The idea

Here’s the catch every logbook faces: your own message often comes sailing back to you as the harbour broadcasts it onward — and on the return trip it wears only its official name, not the working one you scribbled. Write it down again and you’ve logged the same moment twice. The rule that saves you is stern and simple: one official name, one line in the book, forever.

Invariant U: every non-null serverUlid is UNIQUE — at most one row per message, ever. A returning echo is deduped against the register, not blindly inserted, and a UNIQUE index is the write-level backstop that turns a raced double-write into a caught error rather than a silent duplicate.
Scene 04

Two entries become one, cleanly

The idea

So what do you do when the official record and your hurried chalk line are both in front of you? You don’t scratch out and rewrite — that leaves a smudged moment where a reader might glimpse the entry twice. You pour the harbour’s true details into the line you already have, keep your own page-number so nothing jumps, and do it in one clean stroke of the pen.

W2 collapse: delete the server-keyed row FIRST to free the ULID, then merge its fields into the kept optimistic row — all inside one db.transaction. The row keeps its clientTempId so the list never flickers, and because it is one transaction (Invariant A) no reader ever glimpses two rows for one ULID.
Scene 05

No outbox drawer — just a question

The idea

You might expect a separate drawer for letters not yet sent. There isn’t one, and that’s the elegance. To find what’s still waiting, you don’t open another box that can fall out of step with the log — you just ask the log a question: which lines have no official name yet? Those are your unsent letters, and there’s only ever one place their state can live.

Invariant O — the outbox IS a query, not a table: serverUlid IS NULL AND deliveryState != 'failed'. On reconnect the app drains those first, then history dedups by name — but correctness rides on Invariant U, never on getting the order right.

The whole thing, in one breath

The boat keeps its own true log — and one message is only ever one line.

Give the phone its own store and it reads true away from harbour; give every message one permanent name and its echoes can return a hundred times without ever being logged twice. A line’s identity stays put while the harbour’s truth fills in — no duplicates, no smudged moments, no second drawer to drift out of step.

The reconcile engine ashore decides when to drain and how to order; the log aboard guarantees the truth at rest, whatever order it’s handed. Draw the line at the write boundary — one row per message, enforced by UNIQUE — and even a buggy ordering upstream cannot duplicate a single line.
