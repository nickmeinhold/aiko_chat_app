A five-scene explainer

The bell that wakes the boat

Your phone sleeps the moment you pocket it. So how does a message still reach it? Not by staying awake all night — by listening for a bell that says only one thing: come collect.

Scene 01

The harbour that notices for you

The idea

A message lands while your boat is out of sight, ashore and dark. Someone still has to notice it’s worth waking you for — and that someone is your home island, the harbour that never sleeps and already knows your rules: which channels you muted, when your name is called. It watches the water so your phone doesn’t have to.

Where the decision lives: your home island is the single origin of every notification. It evaluates the notify rules — mentions, mutes, unread counts — right where it already holds your account. Nothing on the phone had to stay awake to know a bell is due.
Scene 02

A doorbell, not a delivery

The idea

Here is the whole trick. The harbour doesn’t sail the message out to your dark boat — it just rings a bell across the water. The ring carries no cargo: no words, no sender, only “there’s something for you, come in.” Your boat hears it, wakes, and sails back to the harbour to collect the mail itself. The phone never had to stay awake to be reachable — it only had to listen for a bell.

The pattern, named: wake-then-fetch. The bell is a tiny push that wakes a suspended phone; the phone then fetches the real message from its own home island. A doorbell says come to the door — it doesn’t hand you the parcel through the letterbox. That is why a sleeping phone stays perfectly reachable.
Scene 03

One bell tower everyone rings through

The idea

There’s a catch the sea imposes. No island can ring your particular phone on its own — only one bell tower on the whole coast is allowed to, and it holds the single key the phone will answer to. So every island, yours or a stranger’s across the water, doesn’t ring your boat directly: it asks the one tower to ring for it. Everything else stays spread across the islands; only the ringing is gathered into one place, on purpose.

The one deliberate centre: the push relay is the single component that holds the platform keys — APNs / FCM, welded to one app. It doesn’t hold your account, your channels, or your history; those stay federated across islands. It is the smallest possible centre the phone platforms force, and no smaller is possible.
Scene 04

The ring carries no cargo

The idea

Because every island rings through that one shared tower, you’d never want it to overhear your mail. It doesn’t have to. What crosses the tower is a sealed slip with almost nothing on it — just “something waits, three unread” — enough to wake the right boat and no more. The words themselves never leave your island. Your woken phone sails home and reads them there.

Privacy by shape: the notification envelope carries only an event reference and a count — the event_id_only default. Sender and body never transit the relay; the phone fetches them from its own home island. The one shared centre is deliberately kept too empty to leak.
Scene 05

Built so any island can ring

The idea

Today there’s one island and it rings its own bell — simple. But the shape is drawn for a coast full of islands from the start. A stranger’s island, homing a channel you’re in, rings your boat exactly the same way: it fills in the same little slip and hands it to the same tower. Nothing about the phone changes when the second island appears — the seam was already there.

Federation-ready by shape: every island speaks the one notification envelope, and wake-then-fetch is identical no matter which island sent the ring. Adding islands never rewrites the phone — a new one just points its ring at the same relay. Drawn right once, at the cheapest moment, when there was nothing to migrate.

The whole thing, in one breath

The bell rings. The boat comes in. Nobody has to stay awake.

Your home island watches and decides; one shared tower rings the bell it alone is allowed to ring; the ring carries no cargo, only “come collect”; and your phone wakes, sails home, and reads the message there. Reachable without ever staying awake — and drawn so any island, one day, can ring the same bell.

Only the ringing is gathered into one place; who you are and what you said stay spread across the islands. That deliberate split — one small shared centre, everything else sovereign — is the same shape the rest of aiko chat is built to: the islands, and how they hang together.
