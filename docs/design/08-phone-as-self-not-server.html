Design 08 — The phone is the self, not the server

"Make phones the servers so we don't need a server" is the right instinct aimed at the wrong node. A daily-driver iPhone cannot be a server — the OS and the network forbid it — but it can be the one thing a server can never be: you. This design splits the three jobs a server does across two nodes — the phone carries identity and live presence, an always-on node carries durability and reachability — and shows why aiko's single-home total order makes that split cleaner than Briar, Tox, or SimpleX manage, instead of forcing the multi-master mess that "every phone is a server" actually implies.

Status: vision / exploration, v2 platform-tier — NOT a build spec, NOT on the launch path. Captured 2026-06-30 (task #38) from the question "should we make phones servers so we don't even need a server?" Completes the federation set: companion to Design 06 (sovereign identity — the layer this design puts on the phone), Design 04 (the B4 reconcile engine this design reuses instead of replacing), and Design 07 (the push relay that turns out to be the doorbell the whole thing depends on).

1. The thesis — the phone was never the server

The dream is "no server." The achievable, and actually better, version is: no server that knows who you are, and no server you depend on for durability. You get there not by making the phone host your channels — the OS won't allow it and it would drag Matrix's multi-master complexity back in — but by moving identity onto the phone and leaving durability on a node that is good at being awake. The phone holds the self; the node holds the waiting.

"Every phone is a server" sounds like maximal sovereignty, but follow it one step: if your channel lives on your phone, then your phone is the ordering authority for that channel. When it sleeps — which on iOS is nearly always — the channel freezes; no one can post. When it's lost, the history is gone unless it was replicated; and replication across phones is exactly the multi-master, state-resolution problem aiko deleted to beat Matrix (Design 04 § keeps a single total order precisely so there is nothing to merge). So the pure idea doesn't just hit an OS wall — it quietly rebuilds the thing we set out to avoid. The reframe keeps the win and drops the wall.

2. Why the daily phone can't be a server (the iOS wall)

A server's defining job is to accept an unsolicited inbound connection from a stranger while you are asleep. Hold that sentence up against an iPhone and it fails three ways at once:

A server needs…The daily iPhoneWhy
A stable, dialable public address✗Carrier-grade NAT (CGNAT) puts millions of phones behind one IP with no inbound port. There is nothing for a stranger to dial.
To be awake and listening 24/7✗iOS suspends a backgrounded app within ~seconds and closes its sockets. There is no supported "keep a listening server socket alive in the background" API; PushKit VoIP sockets are tightly policed and rejected if abused for general networking.
To hold a message for an offline recipient✗Store-and-forward is always-on durability. A phone that's off can't hold anything for anyone.

Android is more permissive — a foreground service plus WorkManager can keep a process alive — but it costs a permanent notification, fights Doze and OEM task-killers, and drains the battery, all to be unreachable half the time anyway. And aiko is iOS-first (TestFlight). The iOS row alone is decisive: on the primary platform a daily-phone-hosted, externally-reachable channel is not throttled or degraded, it is impossible.

3. "If phones can't be servers, how is P2P possible?"

This is the sharp question, and the apparent contradiction dissolves on one distinction: a server accepts inbound from strangers while you sleep; a P2P peer makes outbound connections while it's awake. A phone is perfectly capable of the second — NAT and iOS both permit outbound. The whole game is built on that asymmetry.

The network capabilityiPhone?Why
Make an outbound connection✓NAT and iOS both permit it — it's how every app talks to anything.
Accept unsolicited inbound✗No public address; the OS kills background listeners. (This is §2.)
Two awake peers form a direct link✓ brokeredBoth dial out to a shared rendezvous, learn each other's public-facing address, and punch matching holes through their NATs (ICE / UDP hole-punching).
Get woken to start that✓ via pushAPNs is the one channel iOS keeps alive when the app is dead — the doorbell that rings in a dark house (Design 07).

How two NAT'd phones reach each other. Neither can dial the other directly. Instead both reach outward to a cheap, stateless coordinator — a STUN server — which reflects back each phone's public-facing ip:port. The two then fire packets at each other at the same moment, and because each NAT now has an outbound mapping open, the inbound packets are let through. This is ICE (Interactive Connectivity Establishment), the exact machinery inside WebRTC and every video-call app. Once the hole is open the link is genuinely peer-to-peer; the bytes touch no server. But it was introduced by a doorman who saw two outbound knocks and held nothing.

The iOS wake problem. Even "both online" is hard when the recipient's app is suspended. How does B's phone know to wake and start hole-punching? A push. The sender (or the rendezvous) sends an APNs push; iOS wakes the app for a few seconds; it dials out, punches the hole, and messages flow. This is why Design 07 turns out to be load-bearing for any P2P story — not a nicety, the ignition.

The honest claim. "P2P with zero infrastructure" was never quite true. There is always a stateless matchmaker (STUN), sometimes a blind relay (TURN, when hole-punching fails), and for a recipient who is truly offline, a mailbox (store-and-forward). What you can honestly eliminate is the server that knows who you are and the server you depend on for durability — not every server in the path.

4. The reframe — three jobs, two nodes

Unbundle what "a server" actually does for a chat, and it's three separable jobs. The pure-phone idea fails because it asks the worst-suited node to do all three. Split them by which node is good at each:

JobBest nodeWhy
Identity — who you are, your signing key, your reputationThe phone (secure enclave / passkey)The key should live where the human and the biometric are. Design 06 already makes identity a sovereign keypair, gateway-independent. Putting it on the phone is the natural home, not a new idea.
Live presence — "I am here now," the low-latency direct linkThe phonePresence is inherently about the device a human is holding. When both peers are awake, the phone can carry the conversation directly (§3).
Durability + reachability — hold the channel's order, hold messages for offline peers, answer at 3amAn always-on nodeThis is the one job a phone is structurally bad at and a tiny VPS / Pi / dedicated appliance is trivially good at. It is the only reason a server exists.

So the architecture is: the phone is the root of identity and the endpoint of the live link; an always-on node is the durability and reachability layer. Crucially, "an always-on node" does not mean "a company's server" — under Design 06 + the islands work it can be your own $5 VPS, a peer's island, or a dedicated device in your home (§7). Sovereignty comes from identity portability + a cheap self-hostable node, which is already the roadmap — not from fighting the iOS sandbox.

5. The load-bearing rule — one ordering authority, always

Direct phone-to-phone delivery is an optimistic accelerator, never a second master. The always-on node remains the sole ordering authority for the channel. A message sent over a direct link is the same optimistic send aiko already does — it gets its real, total-order ULID when the authority sees it, and the existing B4 reconcile engine sequences it on reconnect. No new consistency model. No merge.

This is the single rule that keeps the whole design from collapsing into Matrix. Naively, a direct A→B exchange while both are offline-from-the-island produces messages with no authoritative order — and the temptation is to let the phones order them, i.e. make every phone a master. Don't. aiko already has the right primitive: the app sends optimistically (renders the bubble immediately) and the gateway assigns the canonical ULID; B4 reconciles the optimistic view against the authoritative log on reconnect. A direct link is just that same optimistic path with an earlier hop — B sees the message sooner, but neither phone is the authority. When either reconnects to the island, the message is submitted, ordered, and B4 does what it already does.

This is the exact parallel to Design 06's "channels stay single-homed." 06 keeps one authority per channel across gateways; 08 keeps one authority per channel across the phone fast-path. Same invariant, different axis. It is why the hybrid is more aiko than the pure version, not a compromise of it.

The one consistency subtlety worth naming

If A→B direct succeeds but A never reaches the island (A's phone dies right after), B holds a message the authority has never seen. Two clean options, both already in aiko's vocabulary: (a) treat B's copy as still-optimistic (a "pending" bubble) until the authority confirms it — identical to today's unacked-send state; or (b) let B relay the message to the island on A's behalf (B becomes a courier, not an authority — the ULID is still minted by the island). Option (b) is strictly better for delivery and costs nothing in the ordering model, because the island still assigns order. plausible — the courier path needs a signature check so B can't forge A's message, but A's sovereign key already signs it, so the island verifies origin regardless of who carried it.

6. The delivery ladder — direct, blind-relay, mailbox

Every "serverless" messenger is really a gradient of fallbacks, and being explicit about the rungs is what keeps the privacy claims honest. From most-private/least-available to most-available/least-private:

⓪ Local mesh — no internet at all — peers in physical radio range talk over local transports that never touch the WAN: same-LAN (mDNS/Bonjour when the router's up but the uplink is down), peer-to-peer Wi-Fi + Bluetooth (iOS MultipeerConnectivity, Android Wi-Fi Direct), or sneakernet (QR, removable storage, à la Briar). Zero infrastructure, zero internet. The catch: only reaches who's near you, and there's no global order until someone reconnects. plausible — see §6.1.
① Direct P2P — both peers awake, hole-punch succeeds. The link is end-to-end; no server sees the content or the metadata. Covers the large majority of NAT pairs. solid
② Blind relay (TURN) — both awake, but hole-punch fails (symmetric NAT, some CGNAT). A TURN server forwards the packets. With E2EE it sees only ciphertext + the fact that two opaque endpoints are talking. solid — this is standard WebRTC fallback; ~10–20% of connections need it.
③ Mailbox (store-and-forward) — the recipient is offline; no direct path exists. An always-on node holds the (E2EE) message until they wake. This is unambiguously a server, and it is the rung the pure-phone dream cannot avoid. In aiko it is the island — which is also the ordering authority (§5), so the mailbox and the order-keeper are the same node. solid

The design's job is to ride as high on this ladder as conditions allow, transparently: local mesh when there's no internet, direct when both are present online, blind-relay when the network is hostile, mailbox when the other side is dark — and to make the privacy cost of each rung legible rather than hidden. Note the elegant collapse at the bottom: because aiko channels are single-homed (§5), rung ③'s mailbox is the channel's authority, so there is no separate "sync server" to run. SimpleX needs disposable queue servers and the relay topology; aiko's mailbox falls out of the island it already has.

6.1 When the internet goes down — and why aiko recovers cleaner than Briar

"Internet down" almost never means "all networking dead" — it means the WAN uplink is gone while local radios still work. Rung ⓪ is the whole off-grid story, and it matters for the cases people actually reach for a resilient messenger: protests, disasters, festivals, censorship, plain dead-zones. Three sub-cases, increasingly austere:

  • LAN survives, WAN doesn't (ISP outage, captive network). Phones on the same Wi-Fi find each other via mDNS and talk directly. Common and almost free.
  • No router at all. Ad-hoc peer-to-peer Wi-Fi + Bluetooth via MultipeerConnectivity (iOS) / Wi-Fi Direct (Android) — the AirDrop / FireChat substrate. Tens of metres, no infrastructure.
  • Never in range at the same time. Custody transfer (the DTN Bundle Protocol term): a mutual peer who moves between you physically carries the message — store-carry-forward, a human mule. Safe because the message is signed by the sender's sovereign key (§5/§9), so the courier can relay but not forge.

And here is the punchline — the §5 invariant pays off hardest exactly here. A local-mesh message is still just an optimistic send (§5): it renders immediately for whoever's in range, and the instant any participant re-touches the island, it gets its canonical ULID and B4 slots it into the one true order — no merge, no conflict, no lost sequence.

Briar is the gold standard for the blackout itself, but it is pairwise sync forever — there is no global authority, so there is no clean "the net came back, now everything converges" moment. aiko's local-mesh messages have a home to go to. So aiko aims for Briar's offline resilience plus a clean online convergence: mesh while dark, and when the lights return, every message that travelled hand-to-hand snaps into a single total order as if nothing was ever offline. That convergence is the thing single-home + B4 buys that a pure-P2P design structurally cannot.

plausible — the radios and frameworks are real and shipping (Multipeer, Wi-Fi Direct, BLE), and the convergence model is just B4 applied to an earlier hop; what's unvalidated is the iOS background/battery envelope for sustained mesh (§8) and the courier UX. The honest limit stays physics: rung ⓪ reaches only who is near, and gives no global order until someone bridges back to the authority. You cannot message the far side of the world with no internet — no design can.

7. The escape hatch — a phone can be a server, just not that phone

The §2 wall is about the daily-driver phone — the one in your pocket, suspended and roaming. A dedicated device is a different animal:

  • An old phone as a home appliance — flashed, plugged in, on home Wi-Fi, app kept foreground/awake. No CGNAT problem on a home connection with a forwarded port or an outbound tunnel; no battery problem on a charger; no suspension if it's the foreground app. This is "a small server that happens to be a phone," and it is the cheapest sovereignty a non-technical user can get: don't buy a VPS, repurpose the drawer phone.
  • A Raspberry Pi / NAS / homelab box — the conventional always-on node.
  • A $5 VPS — exactly what island #2 (enspyr.co) is.

All three are served by the same islands packaging — a small single binary plus its own broker (the island template). So "phone as server" is not wrong, it's mis-scoped: the moment you make it a dedicated node, it stops being a phone-in-your-pocket problem and becomes a deployment-target the roadmap already covers. The interesting, novel work is entirely in §§5–6 — the phone fast-path — not in hosting-on-a-phone.

8. The hard problems (honest map)

NAT traversal still needs a TURN relaysolidNot a flaw, a fact: symmetric NATs can't be hole-punched, so a fraction of connections must relay. A server is in the path for those — blind (ciphertext-only) but present. "Zero servers" is false at the network layer; "zero servers that know you" is the real target.

The both-ends-dark gapsolidIf the recipient is truly off (dead battery, no network, push undeliverable), only rung ③ works. There is no P2P magic that delivers to a powered-off phone. Store-and-forward is mandatory; the question is only whose node holds it.
Battery & wake economicsplausibleEach push-to-hole-punch cycle spins the radio and CPU. Fine for a foreground conversation; punishing if every message wakes the app. Likely policy: direct path only while the app is foreground or recently-active; fall to mailbox otherwise. Measured, not assumed.

Enough to fetch from the mailbox; probably not enough to reliably complete an ICE negotiation + sustained transfer in the background. 9. What this asks of the present — cheap principles

Like 06, the value of writing 08 now is a short list of near-free things that keep it possible, and the discipline to do nothing else yet (it is not on the launch path):

  • Keep identity on the device and gateway-independent. Design 06 + passkeys already do this. The phone-as-self model is free as long as the signing key never becomes gateway-bound. Catastrophic to retrofit; already true.
  • Keep the island the single ordering authority (§5). Don't add any "phone orders messages" path, ever — even as an optimization. Optimistic-send + B4 is the whole story; a second master is the one thing that forecloses this design.
  • Treat the push relay (Design 07) as ignition, not garnish. When notifications get built, the wake-the-app-to-connect capability is the same primitive the fast-path will need. Build it so a push can carry a "peer wants to connect" signal, not only "you have a message."
  • Sign messages with the sovereign key from day one. Origin-verifiability (§5 courier path) needs the message signed by the sender's key regardless of who carries it. If messages are signed anyway (06), the courier and direct paths are unlocked for free.

10. Prior art & research threads

This is a well-trodden gradient; pointing at who sits where on the §6 ladder is more honest than claiming novelty. The novelty in 08 is narrow and real: reusing a single-home total-ordering authority (B4) as both the mailbox and the reconciler, so the phone fast-path needs no new consistency model. Everyone below pays, in some form, the multi-master tax 08 dodges.

Every system above that gave in (Matrix P2P most literally) paid for it in complexity. 08's entire claim to being simpler than the alternatives rests on never crossing it: the island stays the one authority (§5), the phone stays the self. Cross that line and you haven't built Design 08 — you've rebuilt Matrix on a battery.
